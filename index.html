Product engineering, the way I see it

Engineering decisions are product decisions in disguise. These are the principles I keep coming back to.

01

Ship to learn

The fastest way to be right is to put something real in front of people and watch what actually happens. Opinions are cheap; behaviour isn't.

02

The details are the product

Empty states, loading spinners, the copy on a button, the 200ms of animation. Users never see the architecture — they feel the sum of the small decisions.

03

Own the problem, not the ticket

The best engineers understand the why. Give me the goal, not just the spec, and I'll often find a simpler thing worth building instead.

04

Boring tech, interesting problems

Spend the novelty budget where it counts — on the user's problem, not on a stack that's exciting for 20 minutes and painful for two years.

05

Taste is a skill

It's not magic — you build it by shipping, collecting feedback, and paying attention to work you admire. Then you trust it, then you check it.

06

Talk to the humans

Five minutes watching someone use the thing beats five hours arguing about it in a doc. The roadmap is downstream of real conversations.
